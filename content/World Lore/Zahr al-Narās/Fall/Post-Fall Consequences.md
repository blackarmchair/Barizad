Even in ruin, the legacy of Zahr al-Narās endures.
 
The fall of the Brass City did not erase it. Instead, it shattered it—violently yet incompletely—casting its architecture, logic, and metaphysical machinery across the world like flaming debris from a broken star. These fragments did not become inert. They still function, in a manner of speaking. They continue to enforce the last commands they received before the crash, unaware—or uncaring—that the world around them has moved on.
 
Many of these fragments manifest as vast labyrinthian structures; cryptic and paradoxical in nature. Within their warped halls, time may loop, replay, or fracture. Choices vanish once made. Memories become locked gates. In some, a door opened in the past remains closed in the present. Others enforce recursive trials, demanding the same decision again and again until the “correct” one is chosen, regardless of context.
 
These are not traps laid by malice. They are echoes of a city that once governed reality itself. They do not punish—they correct.
 
The Djinn Who Remain  
Among the ruins dwell the surviving djinn, those who once served the Celestine Engine with perfect devotion. Their forms have not all endured intact. Some are cracked like mirrors; others weep fire or flicker through moments like broken lanterns. They still attempt to execute long-dead protocols, enforcing patterns no longer relevant, seeking to “repair” a reality that now resists their efforts.
 
To them, freedom is not a virtue—it is a flaw. Mortal unpredictability is not potential—it is corruption.
 
Their warnings are as terrifying as they are sincere:
 
“_Do not defy the Script. Do not seek freedom. It is not written._”
 
Some fight adventurers directly. Others offer bargains, laced with logic too alien to fully trust. All are dangerous—not because they are evil, but because they are obedient to a system that is no longer whole.
