_As remembered in the "Verses of the Dust-Singers"_
 
They named it Thamurat, Crown of the Sky,  
Forged in brass and burning sigh.  
Upon no mountain did it rest—  
It wore sunlight as gilded crest.
 
Jannat al-Sirr, the wise-men swore,  
A paradise with secret door.  
Where thoughts took shape and shadows lied,  
And mortal fears were cast aside.
 
But proudest name by far, they say,  
Was one no tongue should dare relay.  
Qahirat al-Aqdar, writ in flame—  
The Subjugator’s hallowed name.
 
There dwelt Sirr al-Nar, the Flame Undimmed,  
His voice like bells, his eyes like hymns.  
He drank from dreams, he broke the chain,  
That fate might serve his bright domain.
 
He claimed a forge of glass and stars—  
The Celestine Furnace, vast and far.  
And there he said to ambition sate:  
“Let I be master now of fate.”
 
He bound the future, gagged the stars,  
Sealed prophecy behind brass bars.  
No seers could speak, no visions fly—  
He ruled tomorrow with a lie.
 
Then silence cracked the sky in twain.  
The Wheel of Heaven groaned in pain.  
And like a stone cast from the pyre,  
The city fell in molten fire.
 
Now nothing lives where once it shone,  
But sand that sings and brass half-gone.  
The seers weep, the gods are still—  
And fate rolls on beneath its will.
