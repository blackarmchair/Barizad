# Plot Overview: The War for Fractured Fate

## Core Premise

When the Brass City of Zahr al-Naras fell, it shattered the Celestine Furnace -- the divine engine that burned mortal futures into ordained truth. Now the fabric of reality strains under the weight of too many competing futures. Scattered across the world, the Furnace's prophecy-gems (true dreams) don't merely predict -- they create. Whoever controls them controls the shape of tomorrow.

The PCs are **Dream-Touched**: people whose fates were half-written when the Furnace broke. They exist as living loose threads in the tapestry of destiny -- and that makes them uniquely able to handle the prophecy-gems without being overwritten. Every faction wants them. Every faction fears what they might become.

---

## Act I -- Prophecies Walk (Levels 5-8)

### The Inciting Incident: The Overwritten Village

The campaign opens in a settlement struck by a surfacing prophecy-gem. Reality fractures: people the PCs knew now have different pasts, a loved one ceases to exist, buildings rearrange overnight. The PCs are the only ones who notice the contradictions -- because their fates were never finished.

Investigating the anomaly leads them into their first Fragment Dungeon.

### First Fragment Dungeons

- **The Singing Sands** -- An introductory fragment; teaches the players what true dreams are and what the dungeons feel like.
- **The Vault of the False Caliph** -- Identity collapse, mirror-clones, a trial of truth vs. certainty. The PCs recover their first prophecy-gem.
- **The Crimson Pit** -- A gladiator vault with dream-arenas; escalating combat that introduces the dream/waking duality.

### Faction Courtship

After the first gem is recovered, factions begin circling:

- The **Nisarun** offer to buy the gem outright -- handsomely, and with no questions asked.
- The **Myrian Halls** send a scholar-envoy requesting the gem for study and safekeeping.
- **Aram-Zaneth** agents attempt to steal it. If that fails, they approach with a philosophical argument: fate was a prison, and the gems are the keys to freedom.
- A **Varnathi patrol** arrives claiming sovereign authority over all Brass City fragments, backed by True Brass arms.
- The **Zifari** do not seek the gem. A seer finds the PCs and warns them: every gem used is a future murdered.

Meanwhile, agents of **Sirr al-Nar** -- including the cleric **Issar-Zul, Harvester of Wills** -- are quietly collecting gems of their own. The PCs may hear rumors, find a looted dungeon, or encounter Issar-Zul's handiwork without yet understanding the larger threat.

### Act I Turning Point

Whichever faction the PCs entrust the first gem to **uses it**, and the consequences ripple visibly:

- The Myrians lock it away (safe, but the anomalies continue unchecked elsewhere).
- The Nisarun sell copies of its prophecy to three buyers, igniting a border skirmish.
- The Emerald Courts use it to resurrect a dead star-priest, destabilizing their own government.
- The Varnathi hoard it, and a fragment dungeon near their territory collapses violently.

The PCs realize the gems are not just treasure -- every choice about them reshapes the world.

---

## Act II -- Dreams in Conflict (Levels 9-13)

### Escalation

The true scope of the crisis becomes clear. Fragment Dungeons are no longer quiet ruins -- they're **battlefields**. Other factions send their own expeditions. The PCs arrive at dungeons to find them already breached, or must race rival parties to the gem inside.

### Fragment Dungeons

- **The Bed of the Sleeper-King** -- A sleeping efreet prince whose dreams reshape the dungeon. Waking or killing him may unleash a greater threat.
- **The Maze of Weeping Brass** -- Recursive, maddening architecture. Time loops and impossible geometry.
- **The Observatory of the Starless Astrologer** -- A shattered observatory that still charts fates -- but now charts *all possible* fates at once.
- **The Bazaar of Mind and Hours** -- A marketplace where thoughts are currency and time can be bartered.

### The Contradiction

Two recovered gems contain **mutually exclusive true dreams**. Both cannot be real. If one is fed to the Furnace (or otherwise activated), the other's future is annihilated.

This is where the stakes become personal:

- An NPC the party knows **ceases to exist** -- their future was tied to the erased dream.
- A location the PCs visited is now different, or gone entirely.
- An element of a PC's backstory shifts: a mentor trained someone else, a battle never happened, a homeland changed its name.

The PCs must decide which futures to feed and which to kill. There are no clean answers.

### Issar-Zul Confrontation

Issar-Zul steps out of the shadows. He's been following the PCs, and now he acts directly -- ambush, theft, or a dark bargain. He reveals (willingly or not) that he serves a greater master who is already feeding gems to the broken Furnace. The name Sirr al-Nar surfaces.

### The Zifari Perspective

The **Zifari tribes** become crucial allies (or at least crucial voices). They are the only faction that doesn't want to *use* the gems -- they want to preserve plural futures. Their seers can help the PCs understand what's being lost each time a dream is consumed.

If the PCs have been making ruthless choices, the Zifari may turn against them. If the PCs have been cautious, the Zifari offer guidance toward the third path.

### Act II Turning Point

Sirr al-Nar's influence becomes undeniable. The sky shifts color over a region. An entire settlement flickers between two versions of itself. People lose memories of alternate pasts. Reality is narrowing. He's already fed enough gems to the Furnace to begin rewriting the world in his image.

## Act III -- Ascension or Annihilation (Levels 14-18+)

### The Furnace War

Sirr al-Nar reveals himself openly. His altered Celestine Furnace -- partially rebuilt, fed on stolen dreams -- is attempting to overwrite fate wholesale. His vision: a new Brass City, with himself as the architect of all destiny.

The factions that courted the PCs now **demand** allegiance or declare them enemies. The political web collapses into open conflict.

### Final Fragment Dungeons

- **The Sea of Mirrors** -- A vast reflective labyrinth; every surface shows a different possible future.
- **The Golden Rider** -- A mounted efreet champion frozen between moments; freeing or destroying him unlocks the penultimate gem.
- **The Atrium of the Threefold Wish** -- The last and most dangerous fragment. Three possible futures are offered; only one can survive.

### The Four Paths

The PCs face a final choice. The last dungeon is the **Celestine Furnace itself**.

**Path 1: Restore Fate (Myrians / Emerald Courts)**
Feed all remaining gems to the Furnace and repair it. Destiny returns to the world as it was -- deterministic, orderly, suffocating. The gods regain their old power. Free will becomes an illusion again. The world is stable, but the cost is freedom.

**Path 2: Destroy Fate (Aram-Zaneth / The Ember-Saint)**
Shatter the Furnace permanently. No more prophecy, no more ordained futures. Pure chaos -- liberating and terrifying. The world is free, but the cost is coherence. Reality may slowly unravel without any anchoring force.

**Path 3: Claim Fate (Sirr al-Nar / Varnash)**
Seize the Furnace and impose your own vision. Become the new architects of destiny. The world is shaped by mortal (or efreeti) will -- but whose? Power corrupts. This is the tyrant's path, even with good intentions.

**Path 4: Free Zaneth -- Forge a New Destiny**
The hardest path. Deep beneath the Furnace's ruins, the god Zaneth -- the Scholar of Ashes, the First Blasphemer -- still burns in chains. His heresy was never destruction or tyranny: he believed fate could be *edited*, not imposed.

If the PCs have collected enough gems and can reach the Furnace's heart, they can feed the gems to the Furnace alongside Zaneth's unbound will. This creates a world where:

- Prophecy exists but can be defied
- Destiny is a suggestion, not a chain
- The future is authored by trembling hands, not burning machines

This path requires opposing Sirr al-Nar *and* the Aram-Zaneth (who worship Zaneth but have distorted his teachings into something more extreme). It means trusting that a chained god's philosophy survived his imprisonment uncorrupted.

It is the thesis of the entire campaign: **the right to choose your own future.**

### The Final Battle

**Sirr al-Nar, The End of All Tales** (CR 18) stands at the heart of the Furnace. He has rewritten his own destiny to be unkillable within the Furnace's light. The PCs must either:

- Disrupt the Furnace's power source to weaken him before engaging
- Turn his own fed dreams against him (using gems they've preserved)
- Reach Zaneth's prison beneath the Furnace while holding Sirr al-Nar at bay

The battle is not just physical -- it is metaphysical. The terrain shifts as competing futures collapse. The PCs' past choices echo through the fight: NPCs they saved (or lost) appear as phantoms, fates they preserved (or murdered) manifest as environmental effects.

## Recurring Themes

### The Cost of Choice
Every gem used is a future murdered. Every prophecy fed to the Furnace erases possibilities. NPCs vanish. Locations change. The PCs' own histories shift. The campaign should make players *feel* the weight of deciding which futures live and which die.

### Nobody Is Simply Wrong
The Myrians aren't foolish for wanting order. The Aram-Zaneth aren't evil for wanting freedom. The Varnathi aren't greedy for wanting control. Even Sirr al-Nar believes he's saving the world from chaos. The only purely destructive actors are those who refuse to acknowledge what they're destroying.

### Fate vs. Freedom
The Celestine Furnace was a machine that made the world make sense -- at the cost of everything that makes life worth living. Its absence has made the world free -- at the cost of everything that makes life survivable. The campaign asks: is there a middle ground? Can you have meaning without tyranny?

## Key NPCs

| Faction | NPC | Role |
|---------|-----|------|
| Aram-Zaneth | **Issar-Zul, Harvester of Wills** (CR 16) | Field operative and direct antagonist. Cleric of Zaneth. Shadows the PCs in Act I, confronts them in Act II. |
| Sirr al-Nar | **Sirr al-Nar, The End of All Tales** (CR 18) | BBEG. Fallen Harmonist rebuilding the Furnace under his control. Reveals himself in Act III. |
| Emerald Courts | **Naqqara, Voice of the Hollow Star** (CR 10) | Heretic dream-caster from the Cult of the Hollow Star. Approaches the PCs as a researcher, not a demander. Most likely NPC to be overwritten in Act II -- her loss should hurt. |
| Varnash | **Satrap-Commander Davresh Kol-Vahni** (CR 12) | Mirror Guard officer. Politely demands gems under sovereign authority. Honorable antagonist or strong ally. His missing brother ties into the overwriting mystery. |
| Myrian Halls | **Tahira Kel-Sethune, Lens-Binder of Ur-Mereth** (CR 9) | Tablet-Keeper scholar. The most reasonable faction contact. Can detect timeline alterations. Secretly suspects the Myrian bloodline itself is a Furnace fabrication. |
| Nisarun | **Captain Iyelet of the Twice-Sold Wind** (CR 11) | Rogue/bard wind-ship captain. Charming, useful, and will sell the PCs out if the price is right -- unless they help her find her missing sister Mevra, overwritten by a gem. |
| Zifari | **Khelash the Unremembered** (CR 8) | Druid / Caller of the Wind Shrines. Last surviving member of his Roaming Constellation. The moral compass. His Wind Shrine ability can temporarily restore overwritten memories. |
| Efreeti Warlords | **The Ember-Saint** (CR 15) | Rogue efreet philosopher who wants to burn what remains of fate. Potential ally against Sirr al-Nar, but will destroy gems if given the chance. His Fate-Scar nullifies fate-effects. The campaign's central question made flesh. |
