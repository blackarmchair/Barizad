## How Gems Work

Each prophecy-gem contains a "true dream" -- a fragment of the future as the Celestine Furnace once ordained it. But the Furnace shattered mid-firing. Its last cycle was incomplete, and the dreams it scattered are **partial, overlapping, and mutually exclusive**.

### Activation

A gem's dream is imposed on reality when a faction or individual performs a significant ritual, ceremony, or act of will using the gem as a focus. This is not casual -- it requires intent, preparation, and usually a site of power (a fragment dungeon's heart, a sacred temple, a ley-line convergence). The dream then *leaks* into the world, reshaping reality in the gem's image. The effect is regional at first but permanent, and grows stronger over time.

When an activated gem contradicts another gem, the contradicting gem **goes dark**. Its dream becomes impossible. People, places, and histories tied to the erased future vanish or change. The world *reorganizes* around the surviving dream as though the erased one was never possible.

### Indestructibility

**Prophecy-gems cannot be destroyed.** They cannot be smashed, burned, dispelled, disjoined, or wished away. They are fragments of the Celestine Furnace's last firing -- shards of an engine that once governed reality itself. No mortal or divine force outside the Furnace can unmake them.

A gem can only leave play in two ways:

1. **Contradiction.** Its opposing gem is activated, annihilating the future this gem encodes. The gem goes dark -- inert, cold, and empty. It becomes a dead stone.
2. **The Furnace.** The Celestine Furnace is the only place in existence where gems can be manipulated with full control -- activated precisely, combined, suppressed, or unmade. This is what makes the final dungeon the most important location in the campaign. It is the only place where the rules governing the gems can be *changed*, not merely obeyed.

This means:
- Gems cannot be disposed of to avoid hard choices. If the PCs hold a gem, they carry its future with them -- live and volatile.
- Factions cannot simply destroy gems they don't like. The only way to kill a dream is to *empower its opposite*, which always costs something.
- Sirr al-Nar's access to the Furnace is what makes him the most dangerous actor in the world. He is the only one who can use gems with precision rather than brute activation.
- The Zaneth liberation path requires the PCs to bring gems *to* the Furnace and use it themselves -- which means wresting control of the one place that matters from Sirr al-Nar.

### Superposition

Gems that are held, studied, or hidden remain in **superposition** -- their futures are possible but not locked in. A gem in superposition leaks minor effects (strange dreams, local anomalies, personality shifts in nearby NPCs) but does not reshape reality at scale. This gives the PCs a reason to resist faction pressure: holding a gem preserves possibility, while activating it forecloses it.

However, superposition is not stable. The longer a gem is held without activation, the more its ambient effects intensify. By Act III, carrying multiple unresolved gems should feel like holding a bundle of lit fuses.

## How Contradictions Work

When two gems contain futures that cannot coexist, **activating one annihilates the other's possibility**. The PCs will not always know which gems contradict each other until it's too late. Some contradictions are obvious; others only become clear after one dream has already been activated.

### Rules of Thumb for the DM

- Contradictions should be revealed **gradually**. In Act I, the PCs don't know the gems interact this way. In Act II, they learn the hard way.
- When a dream is activated, its contradiction resolves. The erased future is gone. This is permanent and irreversible outside the Furnace.
- Overwriting should target things the **players care about** -- NPCs they've befriended, places they've visited, backstory elements they've established.
- Not every gem needs to be activated. Gems in superposition preserve possibility -- but the ambient effects get worse over time, and factions will not wait patiently forever.

## Bearer Properties

While carried, each gem confers benefits and drawbacks on its bearer. These properties are a reflection of the future the gem encodes -- carrying a dream of justice makes you more just, whether you want to be or not. A creature can only attune to one gem at a time (attuning takes 8 hours of rest with the gem on your person). Gems radiate strong aura of all schools; CL 20th.

The drawbacks are not curses and cannot be removed while the gem is attuned. They are the dream bleeding into the bearer.

### 1. The Memory of Wind
*A pale blue gem that hums faintly, like a distant song carried on wind.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on Listen and Survival checks. Ignore difficult terrain caused by natural wind or sand.
- **Active (1/day):** Cast *feather fall* or *gust of wind* (CL 8th, DC 14).
- **Drawback:** Your voice echoes ahead of you, as if you are not entirely present in time. -2 penalty on Bluff checks. If you speak a deliberate falsehood, you involuntarily whisper the truth 6 seconds later unless you succeed on a DC 15 Will save.

### 2. The Face of Certainty
*A smooth, colorless gem with a single dark point at its center, like a pupil.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on Sense Motive checks. You are immune to *disguise self*, *alter self*, and similar identity-altering illusions -- you always perceive a creature's true form.
- **Active (1/day):** Cast *true seeing* (CL 10th, self only, duration 1 minute).
- **Drawback:** You cannot willingly disguise yourself, lie about your identity, or use deception to misrepresent who you are. Attempts to do so require a DC 18 Will save; failure means you blurt out your real name and intentions. Polymorph effects still function but feel deeply wrong (-2 morale penalty on all rolls while polymorphed).

### 3. The Hunger for Justice
*A deep red gem, warm to the touch, that pulses like a slow heartbeat.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on attack rolls against creatures that have dealt damage to you or an ally within the last round (a stacking retributive focus).
- **Active (1/day):** Cast *mark of justice* (CL 10th, DC 17). The bearer instinctively knows if a creature within 30 ft. has committed an act of murder or betrayal within the past year (no save; this is ambient awareness, not a spell).
- **Drawback:** You cannot willingly let a witnessed wrong go unanswered. If you see an act of cruelty, injustice, or betrayal and choose to do nothing, you take a -2 morale penalty on all d20 rolls for 24 hours. This penalty stacks with repeated inaction.

### 4. The Dream Deferred
*A brass-colored gem, perpetually warm, that emits a faint glow like banked coals.*

- **Passive:** Fire resistance 10. +2 bonus on saves vs. sleep and fatigue effects.
- **Active (1/day):** Cast *lesser planar ally* (CL 10th) to summon a minor fire elemental or mephit. The summoned creature is loyal but speaks only in Ignan and refers to the bearer as "heir."
- **Drawback:** You dream of the Brass City every night -- vivid, compelling, seductive. After each long rest, Will save DC 16 or become *fascinated* for 1d4 rounds upon waking (staring at nothing, whispering in Ignan). Over weeks of attunement, you begin unconsciously adopting efreeti mannerisms: imperious speech, casual references to "mortals," and growing discomfort in cold or cramped spaces.

### 5. The Verdict Without End
*A grey-green gem veined with black threads that seem to shift like cracks in ice.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on Intimidate checks. *Speak with dead* functions as an at-will spell-like ability (CL 10th), but the dead always answer accusingly, demanding to know why you haven't avenged them.
- **Active (1/day):** Cast *hold monster* (CL 10th, DC 17). The target perceives itself as being judged by an unseen tribunal for the duration.
- **Drawback:** You cannot grant mercy to a defeated enemy without a DC 16 Will save. On a failure, you feel a compulsion to continue pursuing judgment -- demanding confession, imposing punishment, or refusing to accept surrender. Additionally, undead within 60 ft. are aware of you and regard you with unsettling recognition, as though you owe them something.

### 6. The Map of Every Star
*A deep violet gem flecked with points of silver light that shift position like a living star chart.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on all Knowledge checks. You can identify any spell being cast within line of sight automatically (as if using Spellcraft, but with no check required).
- **Active (1/day):** Cast *divination* (CL 10th, no material component, 90% base chance of a meaningful answer). Alternatively, cast *arcane sight* as a free action lasting 10 minutes.
- **Drawback:** You know too much. Whenever you enter a new room, meet a new NPC, or face a new threat, the DM tells you one true thing about it *that you didn't want to know* -- a hidden motive, a future betrayal, an inevitable death. This information is accurate and cannot be ignored. You take a -2 penalty on Initiative (your foreknowledge breeds hesitation) and cannot be surprised (you always saw it coming, even when you wished you hadn't).

### 7. The Price of Tomorrow
*An amber gem with a flaw at its center that resembles an hourglass.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on Appraise and Diplomacy checks. You can instantly determine the exact market value of any item or service, and you intuitively sense when a deal is unfair.
- **Active (1/day):** You may propose a *trade* to the universe: sacrifice one thing you possess (a spell slot, hit points, a memory, a possession) and receive something of equivalent value. The DM determines what "equivalent" means. This is not a wish -- it is a transaction. The universe is fair but not kind. Mechanically: sacrifice up to 5 HP per character level, a spell slot of 3rd level or higher, or a significant personal possession to receive a commensurate magical effect, piece of information, or temporary boon.
- **Drawback:** Generosity becomes impossible. You cannot give anything away without receiving something in return. Gifts, charity, healing without compensation, even sharing food -- all require a DC 16 Will save. On a failure, you instinctively demand or arrange repayment. This applies to healing spells cast on allies (you don't withhold them, but you keep a running tally and the compulsion to collect grows).

### 8. Reflexive Destiny
*A mirror-bright gem with no fixed color; it reflects whatever the viewer most expects to see.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on Will saves. Once per round, when targeted by an illusion spell, you may choose to *believe it into reality* or *disbelieve it automatically* (no save required either way; your choice).
- **Active (1/day):** Cast *major image* (CL 10th) that is partially real -- creatures that believe the image deal or take half damage from it as though it were a *shadow conjuration* (20% real). If you can convince all witnesses that the image is real (Bluff check vs. their Sense Motive), it becomes 60% real for the duration.
- **Drawback:** Your sense of objective reality erodes. You take a -2 penalty on Sense Motive checks (everything seems equally plausible). Additionally, once per session, the DM may declare that something you casually stated or assumed becomes literally true -- for better or worse. ("This place probably has traps" means it now definitely does. "I bet that merchant is honest" means he now is, even if he wasn't before.) The bearer may not realize this is happening.

### 9. The Loyalty That Outlived Kings
*A golden gem, heavy for its size, that feels warm when held against the chest.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on saves vs. compulsion and fear effects. Allies within 10 ft. gain +1 morale bonus on saves vs. fear.
- **Active (1/day):** Swear an oath to protect a specific creature. For 24 hours, you gain +2 on attack rolls and AC when adjacent to that creature and can take a hit meant for them once (redirect a successful attack against them to yourself, as an immediate action).
- **Drawback:** You cannot break a promise. Any oath, vow, or verbal commitment you make becomes magically binding for its stated duration. Breaking a binding oath deals 2d6 Wisdom damage and leaves you *shaken* for 24 hours. You learn to speak very carefully -- or suffer for speaking carelessly.

### 10. The Desire That Could Not Die
*A deep rose-gold gem that is always slightly too warm and never sits still -- it rolls toward the nearest living creature when set down.*

- **Passive:** +2 bonus on Charisma-based checks. You gain a +1 morale bonus on all d20 rolls when pursuing a stated personal goal (declared at the start of each day; must be specific and actionable).
- **Active (1/day):** Cast *suggestion* (CL 10th, DC 17). The suggestion must appeal to the target's desires -- it cannot compel something the target doesn't want. Against a target with a known desire, the DC increases to 19.
- **Drawback:** Satisfaction becomes impossible. When you achieve a goal, there is no emotional release -- only the immediate compulsion to want something more. You cannot voluntarily rest (as in, take a day off or enjoy downtime) without a DC 16 Will save. On a failure, you spend the rest period pacing, planning, and agitating. Mechanically, you do not benefit from "restful" activities that grant morale bonuses, and you take a -2 penalty on saves vs. *exhaustion* from forced marches or sleeplessness.

## The Contradictions

### Contradiction I: Wind vs. Certainty (Act I)
**The Memory of Wind** vs. **The Face of Certainty**

This is the first contradiction the PCs encounter, and the gentlest -- it teaches them how the system works before the stakes become devastating.

| | The Memory of Wind | The Face of Certainty |
|-|---|---|
| **Future** | Plural truth. No fixed identities. The world is a song with many verses. | Singular truth. Every person has one self, one role, one destiny. |
| **Faction interest** | The Zifari treasure this gem. The Nisarun also prefer it (ambiguity is profitable). | The Myrian Halls and Emerald Courts favor this gem (order, lineage, knowable history). |
| **If this dream wins** | The Zifari way of life is validated. Nomadic cultures flourish. But settled civilizations lose the certainty that underpins their institutions -- laws become suggestions, bloodlines become stories, identity becomes fluid. | Settled civilizations are stabilized. Identity crises resolve. But the Zifari way of life is *impossible* in a world of fixed selves -- their oral tradition collapses into a single "correct" version. Roaming Constellations dissolve. |
| **What gets erased** | The Vault of the False Caliph's mirror-clones become real people -- because in a world of plural identity, they *are* real. The dungeon's horror becomes the new normal. | Khelash's lost Roaming Constellation is confirmed dead, not overwritten -- because in a world of fixed identity, they can't exist in two states. His hope dies. |

**DM Note:** This contradiction is best introduced at the end of Act I. The PCs have both gems. A Zifari seer and a Myrian scholar both explain what the gems mean. The PCs don't have to resolve it immediately -- but factions start pressuring them.

---

### Contradiction II: Justice vs. Forgiveness (Act II, Early)
**The Hunger for Justice** vs. **The Verdict Without End**

These two gems seem like they should agree -- both care about wrongdoing. But they encode fundamentally incompatible visions of justice.

| | The Hunger for Justice | The Verdict Without End |
|-|---|---|
| **Future** | Justice is resolution. Wrongs are answered, then *finished*. The guilty are punished and the cycle ends. The living move on. | Justice is eternal. The guilty are never released. Judgment is ongoing, recursive, without mercy or closure. The dead cannot rest. |
| **Faction interest** | Varnash favors this gem (wrongs answered, then power consolidated). The Emerald Courts also lean this way (soul-alignment requires closure). | Aram-Zaneth favors this (eternal suffering as sacrament). Issar-Zul specifically wants this gem to empower sacrificial rites. |
| **If this dream wins** | The Maze of Weeping Brass falls silent -- the brass ghosts finally rest, the murder-mystery resolves, and the inquisitor shuts down. The dead throughout the world begin finding peace. But some debts go unpaid. Some wrongs are *settled for*. | The ghosts in the Maze intensify -- and the effect spreads. Across the world, the dead begin rising to demand judgment. Ancient grievances resurface. Naqqara's "unaligned dead" in Asmarat become actively hostile, demanding their souls be properly weighed. The Emerald Courts' afterlife crisis becomes an undead crisis. |
| **What gets erased** | Aram-Zaneth's blood-rite sacrificial magic weakens -- their rituals depend on suffering that *persists*. Issar-Zul loses access to key abilities fueled by eternal torment. This makes him desperate and more dangerous. | A village the PCs helped earlier -- where they resolved a wrong and brought peace -- is undone. The conflict they settled reignites. The people they saved are now trapped in an endless cycle of grievance. The PCs' good deed is erased. |

**DM Note:** This contradiction forces the PCs to define what justice means to them. There is no clean answer. Both futures are partly right.

---

### Contradiction III: The Sleeper vs. The Rider (Act II, Mid)
**The Dream Deferred** vs. **The Loyalty That Outlived Kings**

Both gems encode futures where the old order persists -- but they disagree on *who* the old order serves.

| | The Dream Deferred | The Loyalty That Outlived Kings |
|-|---|---|
| **Future** | The efreeti princes of Zahr al-Naras awaken and reclaim their thrones. The Brass City rises. The old masters return. | Duty endures beyond the masters. The servants, soldiers, and faithful inherit the legacy -- not the kings themselves. Loyalty to the *idea* outlives loyalty to the *ruler*. |
| **Faction interest** | Sirr al-Nar wants this gem above all others. The efreeti warlords (Sultana Hadranah especially) also covet it. | The Myrian Halls favor this (their Sky-Born heritage is a form of inherited duty). Davresh Kol-Vahni resonates with it personally. |
| **If this dream wins** | The sleeping efreet prince in the Bed of the Sleeper-King awakens -- and he is not grateful. He considers the mortal world a failed experiment and begins reasserting efreeti dominion. Sirr al-Nar gains a powerful ally (or rival). | General Safraz in the Golden Rider dungeon becomes sympathetic -- a soldier who served faithfully long after his kingdom fell. His loyalty is tragic but noble. The efreeti warlords, however, lose their claim to divine sovereignty. They become *servants* again, not masters. The Ember-Saint finds this future intriguing. |
| **What gets erased** | Davresh Kol-Vahni's missing brother is confirmed to have been *reclaimed* -- rewritten into an efreeti servant, not killed. Davresh can find him, but he's no longer human. This is worse than death for a Varnathi. | The Bed of the Sleeper-King becomes a tomb. The efreet prince dies in his sleep, permanently. His dream-shaped dungeon collapses. Any treasures, lore, or allies the PCs hadn't yet claimed from that dungeon are lost forever. |

**DM Note:** This is the contradiction that reveals Sirr al-Nar's agenda most clearly. If the PCs still hold The Dream Deferred, he will send everything he has to take it.

---

### Contradiction IV: The Great Triad (Act II, Late)
**The Map of Every Star** vs. **Reflexive Destiny** vs. **The Price of Tomorrow**

Three gems. Three incompatible epistemologies. Only one can survive. This is the crisis point of Act II.

| | The Map of Every Star | Reflexive Destiny | The Price of Tomorrow |
|-|---|---|---|
| **Future** | Total knowledge. All futures are visible. Nothing is hidden. Choice is informed but paralyzed -- when you can see every outcome, deciding becomes impossible. | Consensus reality. What people believe becomes true. Faith shapes the world. Knowledge is irrelevant if enough people disagree with it. | Transactional reality. Everything has a price. Nothing is free, nothing is forbidden, and the universe runs on exchange. Truth is what you can afford. |
| **Faction interest** | Myrian Halls (knowledge as highest virtue). Tahira Kel-Sethune would sacrifice almost anything for this gem. | Emerald Courts (their entire religion runs on collective belief). Aram-Zaneth (belief as power). | Nisarun (trade as the engine of civilization). Captain Iyelet instinctively understands this future. |
| **If this wins** | Tahira's suspicion is confirmed on a cosmic scale: the Myrian bloodline *was* a Furnace fabrication. But now everyone can see it. The Myrian Halls shatter as their founding myth is exposed. Tahira gets the truth she sought and it destroys everything she loves. | Naqqara's fear is realized: the Mirror Falcon isn't blind -- it's whatever people *believe* it is. The Emerald Courts' religion becomes literally true, but only because enough people believe it. Faith is validated and hollowed out simultaneously. | Iyelet's world wins. But the cost is that Mevra -- her sister -- is recoverable only through trade. Someone must give up their own future to buy Mevra's back. The universe offers this deal with perfect fairness and zero mercy. |
| **What gets erased (if another wins)** | If Reflexive Destiny wins: Tahira's Chronicle Sight stops working. In a world shaped by belief, objective detection of timeline changes is meaningless. She becomes unable to do her job. She is not erased, but her purpose is. | If The Map wins: The Emerald Courts' soul-alignment rituals are exposed as self-fulfilling prophecies, not divine truth. The Cult of the Hollow Star is vindicated, but so thoroughly that the Courts' entire population enters a crisis of faith. Mass unrest. | If either other gem wins: The Nisarun's trade routes in dream-gems collapse -- their commodities become either fully transparent (no information advantage) or belief-dependent (no stable pricing). Iyelet's ship loses its contracts. The Concord of Keels faces economic ruin. |

**DM Note:** This triad is the most complex choice in the campaign. Do not rush it. Let the PCs sit with all three gems, talk to Tahira, Naqqara, and Iyelet, and understand what each future means. If possible, make them resolve it under time pressure -- Sirr al-Nar is closing in and they cannot hold all three gems safely.

The PCs *can* choose to hold one or more of the three in superposition rather than activate any. This preserves possibility but means carrying increasingly unstable gems into Act III -- and factions will try to force their hand. There is no right answer. That's the point.

---

### Contradiction V: Desire vs. Duty (Act III)
**The Desire That Could Not Die** vs. **The Loyalty That Outlived Kings**

If The Loyalty That Outlived Kings survived Contradiction III, it faces a final test.

| | The Desire That Could Not Die | The Loyalty That Outlived Kings |
|-|---|---|
| **Future** | Wanting is the engine of reality. Ambition reshapes the world. Those who desire most fiercely shape what comes next. | Duty is the engine of reality. Those who serve most faithfully preserve what matters. |
| **If this dream wins** | The Atrium of the Threefold Wish's bound genie, Zhadir the Ungranted, is freed -- and becomes a roaming force of chaotic wish-granting across the world. Every creature's deepest want begins to manifest, uncontrolled. | General Safraz, if still alive, becomes a true ally in the final battle -- a loyal soldier serving the PCs' cause because they proved themselves worthy of service. But the world becomes rigid with obligation. Oaths become prisons. |
| **What gets erased** | Any NPC who chose duty over desire -- who stayed loyal when they shouldn't have -- finds their sacrifice meaningless. Davresh Kol-Vahni's loyalty to his Shahenshar becomes a character flaw, not a virtue. His arc is diminished. | Any NPC who chose desire over duty -- who followed their heart against orders -- finds that choice unmade. If Iyelet chose the PCs over the Concord of Keels, that choice is reversed. She "always" stayed loyal to Nisarun. The PCs lose her. |

## Summary: Contradiction Sequence

| Timing | Contradiction | Gems | Core Question |
|--------|--------------|------|---------------|
| End of Act I | I: Wind vs. Certainty | Memory of Wind vs. Face of Certainty | Is identity fluid or fixed? |
| Early Act II | II: Justice vs. Forgiveness | Hunger for Justice vs. Verdict Without End | Does justice end, or is it eternal? |
| Mid Act II | III: Sleeper vs. Rider | Dream Deferred vs. Loyalty That Outlived Kings | Do the old masters return, or does duty outlive them? |
| Late Act II | IV: The Great Triad | Map of Every Star vs. Reflexive Destiny vs. Price of Tomorrow | How should truth work: known, believed, or bought? |
| Act III | V: Desire vs. Duty | Desire That Could Not Die vs. Loyalty (if it survived) | What drives the future: wanting or serving? |

## Gems That Stand Alone

Not every gem contradicts another. Some are dangerous for different reasons:

**The Memory of Wind** may survive its contradiction with The Face of Certainty if the PCs refuse to resolve it. In that case, it remains a wild card -- a gem that encodes the Zifari vision of plural futures. It is the gem most aligned with the Zaneth liberation path, and the one Khelash will beg the PCs to preserve.

**The Dream Deferred** is the gem Sirr al-Nar wants most. If the PCs hold it through Act III, it becomes a bargaining chip -- or a weapon.

## A Note on the Final Dungeon

By the time the PCs enter the Celestine Furnace, they should hold between 3-6 gems (depending on how many contradictions they resolved and how many they preserved in superposition). The gems they bring determine what futures are available at the end:

- **Restoring fate** requires feeding at least 4 gems to the Furnace in harmony, allowing it to reestablish a deterministic thread
- **Destroying fate** requires using the Furnace to unmake all remaining gems simultaneously, collapsing the engine permanently
- **Claiming fate** requires feeding the gems while imposing a single will on the Furnace's mechanism, making the user the new architect of destiny
- **Freeing Zaneth** requires preserving at least one gem from each resolved contradiction (proof that the PCs chose *carefully*, not greedily) and feeding them to the Furnace alongside Zaneth's unbound will

The Furnace is the key. It is the only place where gems can be used with precision -- activated, combined, suppressed, or unmade. Every path requires reaching it, and every path requires wresting control of it from Sirr al-Nar.

The hardest path is Zaneth's because it requires the PCs to have made *thoughtful* choices throughout the campaign -- not maximizing power, but preserving possibility. Players who activated gems carelessly will find this path closed to them. This is by design.
