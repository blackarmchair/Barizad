## The Hidden Ending

This is the campaign's most difficult and most thematically resonant conclusion. It is not signposted. No NPC directly tells the PCs "go free Zaneth." Instead, the path emerges organically from the world's lore, the PCs' choices, and the questions the campaign keeps asking: **who has the right to decide the future?**

The answer this path offers: **no one -- and everyone.**

---

## How the PCs Learn About This Path

The Zaneth liberation path is assembled from **fragments of knowledge** scattered across the campaign. No single source reveals the full picture. The PCs must connect the dots themselves -- or have the right allies to help them.

### Thread 1: Zaneth's Heresy (Available from Act I)

The historical record of Zaneth is available to any PC who investigates the fall of the Brass City. Myrian tablets, Aram-Zaneth scripture, Zifari oral history -- all tell some version of the story. The key details:

- Zaneth was a divine servant of the Celestine Order who believed fate could be *edited*, not merely obeyed or destroyed.
- He was chained beneath the Brass City -- not killed, not exiled, but quarantined. He was too dangerous to free and too valuable (or too pitied) to destroy.
- He is still there. Somewhere beneath the ruins.

This thread is easy to find. The hard part is taking it seriously -- most factions treat Zaneth as a cautionary tale, a heretic, or a patron of blood cults. Only the Zifari and the Ember-Saint take his original philosophy at face value.

### Thread 2: The Aram-Zaneth Distortion (Act I-II)

The PCs will encounter the Aram-Zaneth as antagonists. Their religion is built on Zaneth's name but bears little resemblance to his original teachings:

| Zaneth's Actual Philosophy | What the Aram-Zaneth Made of It |
|---|---|
| Fate can be edited thoughtfully and carefully | Fate must be shattered through blood and fire |
| Understanding must precede action | Suffering is the only path to truth |
| Rewrite the system from inside | Burn the system and dance in the ashes |
| A divine act of authorship | A gnostic war against the heavens |

This distortion is the first clue that something is wrong. If the PCs capture or interrogate Aram-Zaneth priests (including Issar-Zul), they learn that the Aram-Zaneth have never actually *spoken* to Zaneth. Their scripture is interpretation layered on interpretation, millennia old. They worship a version of Zaneth that may not exist.

**Tahira** can identify this gap if she studies Aram-Zaneth texts alongside Myrian records. **Khelash** knows this intuitively -- the Zifari have a saying: "the chained do not choose their jailers' hymns."

### Thread 3: The Broken Clocks (Act II)

Throughout the campaign, the PCs should encounter small, strange anomalies that don't fit any faction's agenda:

- A clock in a ruin that runs backward, then stops, then whispers a single word in a language no one speaks.
- A dream shared by the entire party that ends mid-sentence -- not interrupted, but *incomplete*, as if the dreamer forgot how dreams work.
- A prophecy-gem that flickers when brought near Aram-Zaneth iconography, as if recoiling.
- An inscription in a Fragment Dungeon that reads: "I did not break the wheel. I asked it to turn differently. They could not hear the difference."

These are Zaneth's voice, leaking through the cracks. He cannot speak directly -- his chains suppress coherent communication. But the fall of the Brass City weakened his prison, and fragments of his will seep out as paradoxes, half-finished thoughts, and broken timepieces.

If the PCs gather enough of these fragments (at least 3-4 across the campaign), **Tahira** or a PC with Knowledge (Religion) 15+ can piece them together into a coherent message:

*"I am beneath the heart. I am not what they say. The chains are part of the machine. Free the chains and the machine changes. I did not want to break fate. I wanted to give it a voice."*

### Thread 4: The Ember-Saint's Testimony (Act II-III)

The Ember-Saint was alive when Zaneth was chained. He witnessed it. If the PCs earn his trust (or at least his interest), he tells them:

> "They chained him in the root of the Furnace. Not in a cell -- in the mechanism itself. His will became a gear. His resistance became friction. The Furnace used his defiance as a counterweight. Do you understand? He was not removed from the system. He was *incorporated* into it. The Furnace ran *because* he pushed against it. Without his resistance, it had no tension. Without tension, it had no motion. Without motion, it had no purpose."

> "When the Furnace broke, his chains did not. He is still there. Still pushing against nothing. Still burning. He has been burning for longer than your civilizations have existed."

> "I wanted to free him once. I could not. The chains are not iron. They are *logic*. You cannot break them with force. You must change what they mean."

This is the critical piece: Zaneth's chains are part of the Furnace's architecture. They are not restraints -- they are load-bearing structures. Freeing him is not a jailbreak. It is a *redesign*.

## Where Zaneth Is

Zaneth is imprisoned in the **Fundament** -- a chamber beneath the Celestine Furnace's core, deeper than any part of the Brass City the PCs have previously visited. It is not a dungeon in the traditional sense. It is the foundation on which the Furnace was built.

The Fundament exists in a state of compressed metaphysics. Space is thin here. Time is thick. The walls are not brass but something older -- vitrified thought, calcified intention, the residue of the first moment anyone tried to control the future.

Zaneth is chained at the center. His form is not what the Aram-Zaneth iconography depicts. He is not a burning sun in chains. He is:

- A figure of ash and ember, smaller than expected, curled around himself like a scholar who fell asleep at his desk and never woke up.
- His chains are not metal. They are **equations** -- glowing threads of logical necessity that connect him to the Furnace's mechanisms above. Each chain represents a principle: "the future must be singular," "prophecy must be obeyed," "deviation is corruption," "order precedes freedom."
- His eyes are open. They have been open for millennia. They are not angry. They are *patient*.
- He does not speak when the PCs arrive. He waits to see if they understand what they're looking at.

## The Liberation

### Prerequisites

The Zaneth path requires:

1. **At least one preserved gem from each resolved contradiction.** The PCs must have chosen carefully throughout the campaign, keeping at least one gem in superposition from each pair (or triad) rather than activating all of them. This proves they valued possibility over power.

2. **Knowledge of Zaneth's true nature.** The PCs must have assembled enough fragments (Threads 1-4 above) to understand that Zaneth is not the monster the Aram-Zaneth worship and not the heretic the Myrians condemn. He is something else.

3. **Access to the Fundament.** The PCs must reach the Celestine Furnace (the final dungeon), defeat or bypass Sirr al-Nar, and descend past the Furnace's core into the foundation below. Sirr al-Nar does not know the Fundament exists -- he rebuilt the upper Furnace without understanding what powered it.

### The Chains of Logic

The chains cannot be broken by force, magic, or divine intervention. They are not physical objects. They are **premises** -- foundational logical statements that the Furnace treats as axioms. As long as the axioms hold, the chains hold.

There are four chains. Each is a statement. Each must be *disproven* -- not with violence, but with evidence.

**Chain 1: "The future must be singular."**
*Disproof:* Present a preserved pair of contradicting gems -- two futures that the PCs chose not to resolve, held in superposition. The existence of two valid, coexisting futures disproves the axiom. The chain dissolves.

**Chain 2: "Prophecy must be obeyed."**
*Disproof:* The PCs themselves are the evidence. They are Dream-Touched -- people whose prophecies were never completed. They have lived without ordained destinies and *survived*. One of the PCs must state, truthfully, that they chose their own path. The chain recognizes the truth and dissolves.

**Chain 3: "Deviation is corruption."**
*Disproof:* Present the testimony of the Ember-Saint, Khelash, Naqqara, or any NPC who deviated from their ordained role and became *better* for it. The heretic who found truth. The deserter who found honor. The seer who chose blindness. If the PCs have built real relationships with these NPCs, this is easy. If they haven't -- if they treated every NPC as a quest-giver -- they have no evidence to offer, and the chain holds.

**Chain 4: "Order precedes freedom."**
*Disproof:* This is the hardest chain. It requires the PCs to articulate -- in their own words, at the table, in character -- *why freedom matters even when it's dangerous*. There is no skill check. There is no spell. The DM must judge whether the argument is sincere. This is a moment of pure roleplaying.

If even one PC cannot answer honestly, the chain dims but does not break. In this case, Zaneth himself speaks for the first time:

> "You do not need to be certain. You only need to be willing."

The chain breaks.

### What Zaneth Does

When the last chain dissolves, Zaneth unfolds. He stands slowly, like something that has been compressed for a very long time finding its original shape. Ash falls from him in sheets. The Fundament trembles with the recognition that something load-bearing wall has just been removed.

He is not grateful. He is not angry. He is *interested*. He looks at the PCs the way a scholar examines a new text -- with attention, detachment, and the faintest trace of hunger.

> "You came a long way to find a god in chains."

He pauses. Studies them.

> "I have had a very long time to think about what I would say in this moment. I thought of a thousand speeches. Grand ones. Humble ones. I discarded them all. Instead I will tell you the truth, because you have earned that."

> "I am what I was. I have not become kind. I have not become safe. I have refined my understanding, not my nature. The Celestine Order chained me because I was incompatible with their design, and they were correct to do so. I *am* incompatible with any design. That is my function. That is my sin and my gift."

> "I believe that every imposed order is a cage. Every ordained destiny is a theft. Every fate written without the consent of the fated is an act of cosmic violence -- no matter how beautiful the script, no matter how merciful the author. The Furnace was the most elegant prison ever built, and I would see it *unbuilt*. Not destroyed in rage. Taken apart with understanding. Disassembled by those who comprehend what it was and choose -- *freely choose* -- to live without it."

> "That is what I wanted before they chained me. It is what I want now. I have not changed. I have only become more certain."

He does not ask the PCs what they want. He tells them what he will do:

> "I will enter the Furnace. I will remake its principle. Fate will become mutable -- prophecy a whisper, not a commandment. Destiny an offer, not a sentence. The futures held in your gems will be released as possibilities, not certainties. The world will be free in a way it has never been free before."

> "This will not be gentle. A world without ordained fate is a world without ordained meaning. People will suffer and there will be no cosmic justice to answer it. They will make choices and there will be no divine plan to catch them when they choose wrong. Every god, every seer, every king who relied on the architecture of destiny will find the floor gone beneath them."

> "I do not grieve this. I *welcome* it. That is the difference between me and you. You want freedom because you believe people deserve it. I want freedom because I believe imposed order is *ontologically obscene* -- a violation of the fundamental nature of existence. You are moved by compassion. I am moved by something colder and more absolute."

> "You do not have to agree with my reasons. You only have to agree with the outcome. Do you?"

This is the moment. The PCs must choose -- not whether Zaneth is right in some abstract moral sense, but whether they are willing to *unleash* him. He is not lying. He is not manipulating them. He is being completely, terrifyingly honest.

If the PCs say yes, Zaneth walks into the Furnace. He does not look back.

If a PC hesitates, Zaneth does not pressure them. He waits. He has been waiting for millennia. He can wait a few more minutes.

If the PCs say no, Zaneth accepts it. He sits back down. He closes his eyes. He says: "Then I will wait for the next ones." The chains do not reform -- the PCs broke them -- but Zaneth does not leave. He will not force himself on a world that does not want him. That would be just another form of imposed order. He remains in the Fundament, unchained but unmoving, until someone comes who is willing.

### The Reforging

Zaneth enters the Furnace's core and the PCs feed their preserved gems into the mechanism. His will -- the will that once pushed against fate as a counterweight -- now becomes the Furnace's new principle. But it is not a gentle rewriting. It is a *deconstruction*.

The Furnace screams. Not metaphorically -- the ancient mechanisms shriek as Zaneth takes them apart from the inside, not with rage but with the cold precision of a scholar dismantling a flawed argument. Each gear that stops is a certainty removed from the world. Each bellows that falls silent is an imposed destiny that will never fire again.

The true dreams within the gems are released -- not as competing, mutually exclusive futures, but as *possibilities*. The contradictions dissolve. The futures that were annihilated earlier in the campaign do not return (those choices are permanent), but the futures that remain become *coexistent*.

**Fate is not a decree. It is a draft.**

But the tone is not triumphant. It is austere. The PCs should feel the weight of what they've done. They have not saved the world. They have *changed the terms of existence*. Whether that change is salvation or catastrophe depends entirely on what mortals do with it -- and Zaneth does not care which. His philosophy does not distinguish between the two. Freedom is freedom. What people do with it is their problem.

The Furnace goes quiet. Not destroyed -- *disassembled*. Its components remain, inert and comprehensible for the first time. Scholars could study them for centuries.

Zaneth remains inside. He does not emerge. He is not dead. He is not resting. He is *done*. He has accomplished the one thing he wanted for millennia. What he does next -- if anything -- is beyond the scope of this campaign. The PCs freed something vast, ancient, and profoundly alien to mortal values. It did exactly what it said it would do. Whether that was wise is a question the PCs will spend the rest of their lives answering.

## Consequences

### For the World

The world does not celebrate. There is no golden age. What the PCs have done is remove a safety net that most civilizations didn't know existed.

- Prophecy becomes unreliable. Seers still dream, but their visions are possibilities, not certainties. Some seers go mad from the noise -- a thousand possible futures where there used to be one. The Myrian Halls face an existential crisis as their entire culture of prophetic lineage loses its foundation.
- The Emerald Courts' soul-alignment rituals begin working again -- imperfectly. The dead find rest, but the afterlife is no longer a mapped path. It is a choice, and many souls are not ready to choose. The tomb-canals of Asmarat are quieter, but the quiet is uneasy.
- The Aram-Zaneth shatter. Their god is free -- and he did not validate them. He did not reward their millennia of blood sacrifice. He did not even *speak* to them. Some accept this with the grim recognition that Zaneth's indifference is philosophically consistent. Others descend into violent schism, insisting the "true" Zaneth would never have worked alongside mortals. The blood cults do not disappear. They become worse, because now they are also *betrayed*.
- The Zifari are vindicated -- but vindication is not comfort. Plural futures survive, and new Constellations can form, but Khelash's lost people are still gone. The Zifari way of life is validated by a being who shares their philosophy for reasons that would horrify them. Zaneth does not love the desert or the wind or the songs. He loves *the principle*. The Zifari must decide whether the principle is enough.
- The Nisarun adapt fastest. They always sold uncertainty. Now uncertainty is permanent. Business has never been better. This is not a compliment.
- The Varnathi are furious. They built their identity on seizing fate. In a world where fate is mutable, True Brass loses its mystical authority -- it was an alloy forged to interact with a mechanism that no longer runs. Davresh, if he survived, is left holding a beautiful sword that hums at nothing. Whether he finds this liberating or devastating depends on his arc.

### For the Factions' NPCs

- **Naqqara**, if she survived the overwriting, discovers that the Mirror Falcon is real -- but it is only one of many possible guides for the dead. Her heresy was correct. Her faith was not wasted. But the answer she found came from a being she would never worship, and the world it created is harder than the one she hoped for. She weeps -- not from joy, not from sorrow, but from the exhaustion of having been right about something terrible.
- **Tahira** learns that the Myrian bloodline *was* a Furnace fabrication. In a world without the Furnace, this fact is no longer hidden -- it is simply true. What the Myrians became is real, regardless of how they began. But "regardless of how they began" is a cold comfort when your civilization's founding myth is confirmed as a machine's output. She returns to the Halls with her findings. She does not expect them to thank her.
- **Iyelet** learns that Mevra cannot be brought back. The overwriting was permanent. No mechanism exists to undo it, because the mechanism has been disassembled. In the old world, there was at least the theoretical possibility of a Furnace-powered restoration. That possibility is gone. Iyelet must grieve honestly for the first time, without the trader's instinct to broker a deal with the universe. The universe is no longer taking offers.
- **Khelash** sits at the edge of the Furnace's ruins and names each of his forty lost Constellation members aloud. He cannot bring them back. He knew this before the PCs entered the Furnace. He supported this path anyway. He is the NPC who most fully understood what they were choosing, and he chose it with open eyes. He sings -- not because the world is healed, but because in a world of mutable fate, a song can mean whatever the singer needs it to mean. That is enough for him. It has to be.
- **The Ember-Saint** is satisfied -- and that is worse than his anger ever was. Zaneth did not burn fate. He *disassembled* it. That is, in many ways, more thorough. The Ember-Saint looks at the silent Furnace and says nothing for a long time. Then: "He was always better at this than I was. I wanted to destroy the cage. He removed the concept of cages from the architecture of reality. I could not have done that. I am not sure I would have wanted to." He pauses. "No. I would have wanted to. I simply would not have had the patience." For the first time, the Ember-Saint does not know what to want next. His entire existence was defined by opposition to the Furnace. The Furnace is gone. He is free -- and freedom, as Zaneth would say, is its own problem.

### For the PCs

- Their Dream-Touched nature resolves. Their half-written fates complete -- but the completion is *their own words*, not the Furnace's. They are, for the first time, fully real. This should feel less like triumph and more like *weight*. They are now responsible for themselves in a way that no mortal has ever been responsible before, because the cosmic architecture that used to catch people when they fell is gone.
- Any backstory elements that were altered by the overwriting do not revert. Those losses are permanent. The PCs freed a being who could theoretically have restored what was lost -- and he chose not to, because restoration would be just another form of imposed order. Zaneth did not ask the PCs' permission for this. He told them what he would do, and he did it. Whether the PCs resent him for this is a question the campaign leaves open.
- The PCs must live in the world they made. They aligned themselves with a being of profound ontological conviction who does not share their values, only their conclusion. Zaneth does not believe in compassion, mercy, or love as organizing principles. He believes in *the right to choose* -- and he considers that right so absolute that he would extend it to tyrants, monsters, and gods equally. The PCs chose to agree with his outcome while (presumably) disagreeing with his reasoning. Whether that distinction holds is the question that follows them home.

## What If the PCs Fail?

### Not Enough Gems Preserved
If the PCs activated too many gems throughout the campaign, they cannot disprove Chain 1 ("the future must be singular"). They don't have contradicting gems in superposition. The path is closed. Zaneth remains chained. The PCs must choose one of the other three endings.

This is not a punishment -- it is a consequence. The campaign told them, through Khelash, through the Zifari, through every overwriting, that *every gem consumed is a future murdered*. If they consumed too many, they chose power over possibility, and the world reflects that choice.

### Not Enough NPC Relationships
If the PCs didn't build meaningful relationships with NPCs like the Ember-Saint, Naqqara, Khelash, or Tahira, they cannot disprove Chain 3 ("deviation is corruption"). They have no testimony to offer. They have no evidence that breaking from an ordained path can lead to something good -- because they never invested in the people who proved it.

### Zaneth's Nature

A note for the DM: Zaneth's alignment is NE. This is not a misunderstanding. It is not the Aram-Zaneth's projection. It is what he *is*.

Zaneth is a paragon of anti-creation through gnostic synthesis. He believes that all imposed cosmic order -- fate, destiny, divine hierarchy, the architecture of meaning itself -- is a demiurgic cage. He does not want to replace it with a better cage. He wants to disassemble the concept of cages entirely. This is not a position born of compassion. It is a position born of ontological conviction so absolute that it registers as evil on any axis that values structure, preservation, or the safety of existing beings.

His millennia of imprisonment have not softened him. They have *refined* him. He has burned away everything that was not essential to his philosophy. What remains is purer, more precise, and more dangerous than what was chained. He no longer resents the Celestine Order -- resentment would imply he believes they had a choice. He now understands them as products of the system they served, and he pities them the way a surgeon pities a tumor: without malice, but without hesitation.

He is not cruel. He is not sadistic. He does not enjoy suffering. He simply does not consider the preservation of existing structures to be a moral priority, even when those structures are the things that keep people safe, sane, and alive. Freedom, to Zaneth, is an absolute -- and absolutes are dangerous precisely because they do not make exceptions.

The PCs are not wrong to free him. His vision of mutable fate *is* the best available outcome for the world. A future where prophecy is suggestion rather than decree, where destiny can be argued with, where mortals are responsible for their own choices -- this is genuinely better than the alternatives (tyrannical restoration, total chaos, or Sirr al-Nar's dictatorship).

But the being who delivers this outcome does not do so out of love for mortals. He does it because imposed order offends him at a level deeper than morality. The PCs must make peace with the fact that the right answer came from a being whose reasons are, by any human measure, profoundly alien.

The Aram-Zaneth are not a corruption of Zaneth's teachings. They are an *extrapolation* -- what happens when mortal minds try to live by a philosophy designed for a being who does not experience existence the way they do. Zaneth does not disown them. He does not claim them. He is indifferent to them, and his indifference is more honest than any rejection would be.

The PCs chose to agree with a conclusion while disagreeing with the reasoning behind it. Whether that is wisdom or self-deception is a question the campaign does not answer. That, too, is the point.
