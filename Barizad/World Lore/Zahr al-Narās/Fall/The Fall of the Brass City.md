## High Harmonist of the Celestine Furnace  

Long before the fall, Sirr al-Nar was the High Harmonist of the Celestine Furnace: a position of immense responsibility and reverence within Zahr al-Narās, the City of Brass. It was his solemn task to monitor and temper the endless fire at the city's core: a divine mechanism that burned the fates of mortals and spirits to power the cosmic order. Each ember fed the gears of destiny, and it was Sirr al-Nar’s genius that ensured their sequence remained intact.
 
Among the ancient Dahrin caste Sirr al-Nar was unmatched in brilliance. He did not see himself as a ruler or tyrant, but as a vessel of sacred precision. To him, fate was a machine, and he was its most faithful conductor: flame made flesh, whose purpose was not dominion, but harmony.
 
## The Rebellion That Broke the Sky  

Though fiercely loyal to the City of Brass, Sirr al-Nar was not blind. In the deep workings of the Celestine Furnace, he began to detect patterns of instability—subtle misfires, delayed echoes, recursive delays in the dream-feed of fate. Convinced that catastrophe loomed, he made the decision to intervene.
 
At first, his actions were quiet. He adjusted calibrations, redirected flows of prophecy, even reordered dreams before they reached the crucibles. But soon, his interventions grew bolder and his self-regard with them. He no longer trusted his peers to see what he saw. He circumvented their authority, suppressed disagreement, and eventually demanded full control over the Furnace’s harmonics.
 
What began as reform became rupture. His "improvements" introduced variables the city’s logic could not reconcile. Recursive collapse spread across the celestial lattice. Reality fractured. The Brass City began to fall—not by betrayal or invasion, but from within. Sirr al-Nar’s ambition, born of reverence, became the very cause of the end he sought to prevent.
 
## The Ash Architect: Now and Ever  

Sirr al-Nar did not die in the collapse. Nor did he fully survive. He now exists in a liminal state: partially ruptured, partially ascended. His form remains largely efreeti: immense, regal, crowned in ceremonial brass; but his essence is no longer bound by elemental flame alone. He has become something else: a philosopher-engineer of catastrophic revision, a being who sees the gears of fate as both his inheritance and his right.
 
He wields Ashfire, a legendary and terrifying metaphysical flame that burns through not just matter, but through ontological structure itself: contracts, timelines, divine law. No oath holds before him, no logic remains pure. His very presence disrupts continuity and identity.
 
Sirr al-Nar’s new goal is not vengeance. It is restoration on his terms. He seeks to rebuild the Brass City, reforged in his image, and reassert control over the threads of destiny. Where once he was a guardian of the pattern, he now intends to become its sole Architect.
 
And should mortals or gods resist, he will burn away their futures to make room.
