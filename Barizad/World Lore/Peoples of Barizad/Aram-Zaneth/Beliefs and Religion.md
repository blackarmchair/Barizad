Central to the faith of Aram-Zaneth is the worship of Zaneth: the Chained Sun, an inverted deity whom they claim was exiled from the Brass City for revealing forbidden truths to mortals. The people of Aram-Zaneth live steeped in symbolism, where nothing is mundane—everything is either sacred or taboo. Their religious cosmology revolves around Zaneth and a cohort of lesser divine beings known as the Thirsting Court, each representing aspects of desire, suffering, and the cyclical nature of truth.
 
They reject the notion of linear time or predestined fate, embracing instead a vision of existence as an eternal cycle of suffering and renewal, powered through blood and sacrifice. Their rituals reflect this worldview: blood rites, ecstatic trances, dream-feasting, and offerings of flame are common, often enacted atop vast, terraced ziggurats stained with centuries of sacrificial blood.
 
The iconography of Aram-Zaneth is as potent as it is unsettling. The Chained Sun, a stylized disc split by black chains and burning from within, is their most sacred symbol—worn as pendants, etched into armor, and painted on altars in blood. The Inverted Tree, whose roots stretch skyward while its fruit lies buried deep beneath the earth, represents hidden knowledge and the reversal of divine order. The Brass Spiral, a complex labyrinthine glyph, is believed to mirror the unraveling of fate itself.
 
Every aspect of their ritual life is infused with dark reverence, designed not to please benevolent gods, but to awaken sleeping truths and draw power from suffering.

## How the Aram-Zaneth View the Dream-Touched PCs

The Aram-Zaneth do not treat the PCs as immediate enemies despite ideological tension. The reason is theological:

**From the Zanethi perspective, the party is reenacting Zaneth's original act of rebellion.** Every fragment the party touches -- restoring a dream, preventing another faction from controlling it, altering the trajectory of prophecy -- is active engagement in the shaping of reality. To a Zanethi thinker, this is not random adventuring. It is living proof that the will of mortals is still capable of altering the cosmic order.

The party becomes something like a **metaphysical experiment** -- evidence that destiny can still be contested. The Aram-Zaneth adopt a posture of **controlled observation** rather than immediate hostility:

- They monitor the party.
- They occasionally assist when goals align.
- They intervene only if the party attempts to fully restore the Celestial Order's deterministic prophecy.

Zanethi NPCs might refer to the party using religious language:

> "You walk where prophecy once stood."
> "You are living proof that destiny can still be contested."
> "Zaneth broke the chains. You test whether they remain broken."

The tone is not admiration. Not hostility. **Intense curiosity.**

Their tolerance has limits. They would likely intervene if the party attempts to fully restore deterministic fate, rebuild the Celestial Order's machine, or destroy Dream-Gems outright -- outcomes that would invalidate Zaneth's rebellion. At that point they may conclude: *the experiment has produced the wrong conclusion.*

## The Self-Refuting Nature of Zanethi Philosophy (DM Note)

The Aram-Zaneth are a pointed critique of gnostic-derived ideologies: fanatical, blood-soaked, unmoored from anything humanizing, and ultimately self-refuting.

The self-refutation operates at a basic level. To say "liberation is the highest good" already presupposes:

1. A **hierarchy of goods** (liberation > other goods)
2. A **standard of evaluation**
3. A **narrative of meaning** in which liberation improves the world

But Zanethi doctrine rejects precisely the structures that make those things possible -- stable value hierarchies, objective standards, teleological narratives. Once those are rejected, the claim that liberation is "good" cannot be grounded. The doctrine collapses into: "Liberation is good because we will it" -- which is not a justification but an assertion of preference backed by power.

Aram-Zaneth depends parasitically on the very order it claims to overthrow. The hierarchy of values that allows them to call liberation good and oppression evil only makes sense within a universe where values are meaningful, narratives have direction, and goods can be ranked. Those are precisely the things the Celestial Order's teleological structure provides.

Their god does not even value them. Their devotion is directed toward a being whose defining trait is radical self-assertion. Zaneth does not disown them. He does not claim them. He is indifferent to them, and his indifference is more honest than any rejection would be.

They worship liberation while binding themselves to an entity that recognizes no obligation to them whatsoever. The ideology claims liberation but produces **nihilistic fanaticism**. The gap between the god and his worshippers is where the tragedy of Aram-Zaneth lives.
