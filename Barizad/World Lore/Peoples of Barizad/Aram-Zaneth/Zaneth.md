1. Titles:
    
    1. The Father of Flame
    2. The Inverted Light
    3. He Who Was Bound
    4. The God-Forged God
2. Cosmic Role
    
    1. Zaneth is not a creator deity in the traditional sense. Rather, he is a _former servant or creation of the Celestial Order_—perhaps an archon, seraph, or automaton of the Celestine Furnace—who rebelled, claiming the gods of heaven fear mortal agency.
    2. According to Aram-Zaneth doctrine:
        
        1. The Celestine Order enslaved time and destiny to maintain their perfect, golden world.
        2. Zaneth fractured prophecy deliberately, allowing mortals to shape it for themselves.
        3. For this crime, he was cast out, shackled in burning chains.
        4. His fall cracked the Brass City—the first blow in its eventual collapse.
    3. Philosophy
        
        1. Zaneth is not worshipped _as a god in the normal sense_—his divinity is the result of apotheosis through struggle.
        2. This theology frames Aram-Zaneth as a gnostic rebellion against celestial determinism
        3. Zaneth represents _becoming_—a fire that devours all imposed form so that "truth" can be forged from ash.
3. Alignment and Domains
    
    1. Alignment: NE
    2. Domains: Knowledge, Fire, Trickery
    3. Typical Worshipers: The people of Aram-Zaneth, Warlocks, misanthropic and dysgenic cultists

## The Typological Relationship: Zaneth and Sirr al-Nar (DM Note)

Zaneth is, in many ways, a type of the classical theological Satan -- not the modern pop-culture figure of rage and sadism, but the Augustinian/Thomistic Satan whose defining characteristic is **radical self-assertion of the will against the structure of reality**. Not chaos. Not cruelty. Prideful autonomy.

| Element | Classical Satan | Zaneth |
|---|---|---|
| Position | Originally part of the celestial order | Servant/construct of the Celestial Order |
| Rebellion | Refuses subordination | Rejects imposed fate |
| Core sin | Pride / self-exaltation | Radical assertion of will |
| Result | Fracture in the divine hierarchy | Fracture in prophecy |
| State | Bound / limited | Chained in the Fundament of Zahr al-Naras |

His evil is not cruelty or hatred. It is the **misordering of the will relative to reality** -- radical self-assertion without reference to any higher order. He does not oppose good. He simply does not recognize it as a category that matters. He is morally unmoored, not temperamentally violent.

**Sirr al-Nar as Counter-Zaneth:** Sirr al-Nar is a typological Zaneth -- he also rebels against the old order, but with a fundamentally different goal. Where Zaneth says "no one should control fate," Sirr al-Nar says "someone must control fate -- and it should be me." He sees himself as completing Zaneth's work, but the completion is imperial rather than liberating.

Sirr al-Nar might say:

> "Zaneth shattered the prison of prophecy. I will forge its chains into a crown."

The final confrontation is therefore the culmination of a cosmic argument that began with Zaneth's original rebellion.

**Why His Worshippers Misunderstand Him:** The Aram-Zaneth interpret Zaneth as a liberator, a teacher, a path to transcendence. But his rebellion was an act of personal self-assertion whose consequences happened to benefit mortals. He did not intend to free humanity. He simply refused the system. The freedom of mortals is a side effect. His followers project a value system onto something that fundamentally rejects the very idea of value.
