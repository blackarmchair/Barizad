> You enter a vaulted chamber of echoing silence. It resembles a grand auditorium — tiered seating curves upward along the back wall, each row lined in alternating bands of brass and black jet. Crystalline chandeliers hang from impossible heights, many dim or shattered. The air smells faintly of heated copper and burnt parchment.
> 
> At the front of the room rises a wide stage of dark obsidian and polished metal, embedded with a strange circular pattern. Upon it stand six humanoid figures wrought from golden-brass alloy — automata, frozen mid-motion like actors awaiting a cue. Their faces are blank masks, etched only with the suggestion of expression.

### Overview

This room can be one of two things:
- A safe place to rest with a strange play stuck on repeat
- A powerful metaphor for the PCs situation, an invitation to accept destiny, and a hold of vast riches

The players must understand the play and take the stage at the appointed time to accept destiny and claim their reward. If they do not, they can rest safely in the auditorium and move-on.

### Performance Trigger
1. When at least half the party sits-down on any of the provided seating the play begins; the automata animate with a low, synchronized click.
2. They begin to perform *Deus Extra Ludos*, an ancient and stylized dramatization of the Fall of Zahr al-Narās.

### Performance Overview
The play proceeds in prophetic language and halting, ritualized movement. It recounts:

1. The founding of Zahr al-Narās, the city as celestial regulator
2. The rise of Sirr al-Nar, praised for his insight
3. The first deviation from prophecy
4. The onset of dissonance
5. The unwritten ending—at which point the automata freeze, unable to proceed

### Opening Lines

> Before the breach, before the flame,
> The wheels once turned in perfect name.
> Each spark a star, each line a law,
> No voice defied, no flaw to draw.
> In brass we sang, in light we moved,
> By script divine, the world approved.
> But one note bent, and thus began,
> The song unwritten by the hand of Man.

### Middle Section
The play recounts the tale of Sirr al-Nar and the fall of Zahr al-Narās

### Final Lines

> The gears did grind, the heavens bled,
> The sun-machine fell, cold and dead.
> The Arbiter broke the written line,
> And twisted brass refused design.
> We speak these lines as we were made,
> Our roles assigned, our motions played.
> But one part waits, still undefined—
> A voice unwrit, a step unlined.
> The script runs dry, the page unturns…
> Who enters now, when no one learns?

### The Riddle
The play is a synecdoche for the PC's situation.

- The play is a pre-written story acted-out by automata; this mirror the position of those bound by fate. But the PCs are not bound by fate and they must choose if they will submit to the will of Zahr al-Narās (accept destiny) or take the path of Sirr al-Nar and reject it.  
- The subtle implication here is that the fall of Zahr al-Narās was fated by Zahr al-Narās itself. The fact that the automata and the play were created before the fall shows that the author had foreknowledge not only of the fall but of the PCs being here, now.  
- This is fate winking at the PCs and inviting them to align themselves to their prefigured destiny; it's up to them if they will or not. Their agency is the central theme.

### Conclusion
If one (or more) PCs decide to take the stage in acceptance of fate the following lines immediately spring to mind and they may choose to perform them:  

> If none shall end what was begun,
> Then let the thread be pulled by one.
> Not forged of brass, nor bound by line,
> But flesh that dares to speak design.
> To the PC who speaks these lines:
> As the final words rise unbidden from your mouth, the air thickens around you heavy with the weight of destiny and ages. The stage does not applaud. It exhales.
> Somewhere in the script, your name is now written.

### The Blessing of Scripted Flame
Granted only to the PC who spoke the final lines:

The Blessing of Scripted Flame (Su): Once per day, as a swift action, the bearer may choose to be “written into” a spell effect they can see, as if the spell had included them as a target, even if they would not normally qualify. For example, the player can insert himself into a haste, teleport, or bless. This cannot affect enemy-only or unwilling-only spells (e.g., disintegrate). Symbolically, this is the PC learning to enter the script of fate by will, rather than construction.

> The next time an opportunity presents itself to use this ability; read the following text:
> You feel it — the spell pulling away from you like a current — and something inside you resists.
> A word comes to mind. A stage direction never spoken.
> “Enter.”
> And before the spell completes, your body moves — not physically, but narratively — and you’re in it.
> You feel the spell take hold of you, though no one had named you its target.
> Next, explain the blessing.
