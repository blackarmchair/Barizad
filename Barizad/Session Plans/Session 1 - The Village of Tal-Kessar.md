**Starting Level:** 5
**Tone:** Creeping wrongness, then sudden dread
**Leads Into:** The Singing Sands (first Fragment Dungeon)

---

## Before the Session

### PC Setup

Each PC should have a **personal connection** to the village of Tal-Kessar. During character creation or a brief pre-session conversation, ask each player to establish one of the following:

- A person they know here (a friend, relative, mentor, contact, debtor)
- A place they've been before (a tavern, a shrine, a market stall, someone's home)
- A memory of something that happened here (a fight, a deal, a celebration, a loss)

These connections will be **targeted by the overwriting**. The more specific the players are, the harder the anomalies will hit.

Also establish (privately, with each player) one detail about their past that feels solid and certain -- a hometown, a parent's name, the outcome of a formative event. File these away. They become ammunition later in the campaign.

### The Dream-Touched Hook

The PCs do not know they are Dream-Touched at the start. They learn this through the events of session 1. What they know is that they've each had the same recurring dream for the past several weeks:

> A vast brass machine, turning in darkness. A sound like a bell cracking. A voice that begins to say something and never finishes. Then silence -- not the absence of sound, but the *removal* of it, as if the dream was edited after the fact.

The PCs have not necessarily shared this dream with each other. If they're strangers, the dream is the thing that draws them to the same place. If they already know each other, it's an unspoken discomfort they've each been carrying.

## Part 1: Arrival

### Tal-Kessar

Tal-Kessar is a small oasis trading village on the edge of the deep desert. It sits at the junction of two caravan routes and survives on water, trade, and stubbornness. Population roughly 300. Mud-brick buildings, a central well, a small bazaar, a shrine to a local wind-spirit, a caravanserai.

It is not important. That's the point. The campaign begins in a place that doesn't matter to empires -- because the overwriting doesn't care about importance. It rewrites whatever is in range.

**Read or paraphrase:**

> The road to Tal-Kessar is the same road it's always been: dust, heat, and the distant shimmer of something that might be water. The village appears on the horizon like a smudge of brown against the pale sand -- low walls, a few palms, the glint of the central well's brass cap.
>
> You've been here before. [Or: You've heard of this place.] It's the kind of settlement that exists because someone had to live somewhere. Unremarkable. Comfortable. Familiar.
>
> Except today, as you approach, something feels slightly wrong. Not dangerous. Just *off* -- like a word on the tip of your tongue, or a room where someone moved the furniture while you were asleep.

### The First Anomalies (Subtle)

When the PCs arrive, nothing is obviously broken. The wrongness is atmospheric and personal. Present these as small, easily dismissed details in the first hour of play:

**The Well.** A PC who has been here before remembers the well cap being made of brass. It is now stone. No one in the village thinks this is strange. "It's always been stone," they say, confused by the question.

**The Innkeeper.** The inn is called the Dust Curtain. The innkeeper, Harath, is a heavyset man with a burn scar on his left hand. Except one PC remembers him as thin, with the scar on his right hand. Harath does not recognize any contradiction. He's always been this way. He is friendly, normal, and *wrong*.

**The Shrine.** The village shrine honors a local wind-spirit called the Breath of Tal. A PC with Knowledge (Religion) DC 12 recalls that the Breath of Tal is traditionally depicted as a veiled woman. The shrine's idol is now a veiled man. The shrine-keeper, old Mirath, insists it has always been male. She is not lying. She believes it completely.

**A Missing Person.** One PC established a personal connection to someone in Tal-Kessar. That person exists -- but they do not recognize the PC. They have no memory of ever meeting them. They are polite but bewildered. "I'm sorry, friend. You must have me confused with someone else."

Or, worse: that person does not exist. No one in the village has heard of them. There is no record of them. The house where they lived belongs to someone else and always has.

**DM Guidance:** Do not explain any of this. Do not call for Sense Motive or Knowledge checks to "detect" the wrongness. Just describe it and let the players react. The dissonance should feel like *their* problem, not the world's. Everyone else in Tal-Kessar is fine. The PCs are the ones who don't fit.

## Part 2: The Wrongness Deepens

### The Evening

As the day wears on, the anomalies escalate. These should accumulate across scenes -- dinner at the inn, a walk through the bazaar, a conversation with a local.

**The Doubled Boy.** A child named Safar is seen playing near the well. An hour later, the PCs see him again -- but slightly different. His clothes are a different color. He's left-handed instead of right-handed. He answers to the same name. Both versions are present in the village simultaneously, and no one notices. If the PCs point this out to a villager, the villager looks uncomfortable, glances between the two Safars, and says: "I... yes. That's Safar. He's always been..." They trail off. They change the subject. They do not look at the two boys again.

**The Rewritten Wall.** A mud-brick wall near the bazaar bears a carved inscription. In the morning, it reads: "Built in the third year of the Kessari drought." By evening, it reads: "Built in the third year of the Varnathi occupation." The wall has not been touched. The carving style is identical. A successful DC 15 Knowledge (History) check confirms: there was no Varnathi occupation of this region. But the wall is very certain that there was.

**The Dream Returns.** That night, all PCs experience the recurring dream again -- but this time, it's different. The brass machine is *closer*. The cracking bell is *louder*. And the voice that never finishes its sentence gets one word further than it ever has before:

> *"Find--"*

Then silence. Then the PCs wake. Each PC must make a DC 14 Will save. On a success, they wake normally. On a failure, they wake with a nosebleed and a single word in a language they don't speak burned into their memory. (The word is Ignan. It means "unfinished." A PC who speaks Ignan recognizes it immediately. Others can identify it with a DC 15 Knowledge [the planes] or Decipher Script check.)

### The Morning After

The PCs wake to discover that the anomalies have worsened overnight.

**The Missing Building.** A building that existed yesterday -- the shrine, a house, a market stall -- is gone. Not destroyed. The space where it stood is empty, undisturbed ground, as though nothing was ever built there. Villagers walk past the gap without noticing. If pressed, they become confused and distressed: "There's never been a building there. Why would there be?"

**The Sand-Song.** A low, musical hum can be heard coming from the desert to the south. It sounds like wind through brass pipes -- but there are no pipes, and the wind is still. The sound is faint but constant. Animals are uneasy. Dogs whine. Camels refuse to face south.

**The Second Safar.** One of the two Safars is gone. The remaining one is alone and frightened. He doesn't remember having a double, but he keeps looking over his shoulder. He tells the PCs, if they ask: "I had a dream about a city made of brass. It was falling. Someone was calling my name but they got it wrong."

### Information Gathering

PCs who investigate can learn the following:

**From Harath (the innkeeper), DC 12 Gather Information:** "The singing started three days ago. Comes from the south. Some of the herders say there's a new ruin out there -- appeared overnight, half-buried in the dunes. I don't go south. Nobody does anymore."

**From Mirath (the shrine-keeper), DC 15 Diplomacy or Knowledge [Religion]:** "The Breath of Tal has gone silent. The shrine gives no blessings. The wind-prayers return unanswered. Something is *sitting* on the desert out there, something heavy, and it is pressing down on what is true."

**From a passing caravan merchant, DC 10 Gather Information:** "There's a ruin in the dunes, two hours' walk south. Wasn't there last week. Brass walls sticking out of the sand like ribs. The sand around it *sings*. My camels won't go near it. I saw a man walk toward it yesterday. He hasn't come back."

**From Safar (the child), no check needed:** "The dream-city was falling and there was a man made of fire and he was *angry*. And there was a sound like... like when you break a bell. And I think the city wanted to tell me something but it forgot what it was."

## Part 3: The Ruin in the Dunes

### The Journey South

The walk south takes roughly two hours. The terrain shifts from hard-packed caravan road to open desert. As the PCs approach the source of the singing, describe the following progression:

**First 30 minutes:** Normal desert travel. The hum grows slightly louder. It sounds like a single sustained note, somewhere between a chant and the ringing of struck metal.

**One hour:** The sand changes color. It's subtly *wrong* -- flecked with tiny grains of brass that catch the light. The hum has harmonics now. It sounds like two or three notes layering over each other, not quite in tune. The PCs feel an urge to hum along. This is not magical compulsion -- it's just oddly compelling. If a PC does hum along, they feel a brief flash of warmth and hear, just for a moment, a snatch of a language they don't speak. Then it's gone.

**90 minutes:** The sand is now visibly mixed with brass filings. The singing is loud enough to be uncomfortable, like standing inside a bell tower. The PCs can see it: a structure jutting from the dunes, half-buried.

### The Entrance to the Singing Sands

**Read or paraphrase:**

> It rises from the sand like a broken finger pointing at the sky: a wall of tarnished brass, fifteen feet high, inscribed with symbols that shimmer and shift when you look at them sideways. The sand around it is sculpted into dunes that don't look natural -- too regular, too symmetrical, as if something beneath the surface is imposing order on the landscape.
>
> The singing is coming from inside. It reverberates through the brass walls and into the ground beneath your feet. Up close, it doesn't sound like music anymore. It sounds like a voice -- vast, inhuman, and infinitely patient -- reciting something over and over in a language that was old when the world was young.
>
> There is an opening. A doorway, clear of sand, as if it was uncovered yesterday. Or as if the sand is being *pushed back* by whatever is inside.
>
> Beyond the doorway: darkness, the smell of hot metal, and the faintest draft of air that is far too warm for a ruin buried in sand.

This leads directly into Room 1 of The Singing Sands dungeon.

## Part 4: After the Dungeon

When the PCs emerge from the Singing Sands with the Memory of Wind gem, they return to find Tal-Kessar has changed again.

**Option A (if the PCs were gone less than a day):** The village is mostly intact, but another building is gone and a villager the PCs spoke to yesterday now has a different name and doesn't remember them. The anomalies are accelerating.

**Option B (if the PCs were gone more than a day):** The village is stable -- the singing has stopped, the anomalies have frozen in place. The rewritten versions of people and places are now permanent. The Safar who disappeared is simply gone. The person who didn't recognize the PC still doesn't. The well cap is still stone. This is the world now. The gem's removal from the ruin stabilized the area, but it didn't *fix* anything. What was overwritten stays overwritten.

**In either case:** The PCs now hold a prophecy-gem. They can feel it -- a faint hum, a warmth, a sense of *potential*. And within a day or two of their return, the first faction contacts arrive.

### First Contact: Khelash

Before any faction makes a play for the gem, **Khelash the Unremembered** finds the PCs. He is sitting at their campfire when they return, or waiting at the inn, or simply walking beside them on the road as though he's always been there.

He does not ask about the gem. He tells them a story:

> "I had a family once. Forty of us. We walked the deep desert together and we sang the wind into shape. One morning I woke up and they were gone. Not dead. Not taken. *Gone.* The tents were there. The fires were cold. The tracks in the sand led nowhere. I am the only one who remembers them. Even the wind has forgotten their names."

He pauses. Looks at the gem.

> "That thing you carry is a dream the world once had about itself. Be careful what you do with it. Every dream that comes true is another dream that dies."

He does not ask for the gem. He does not offer advice. He shares food if they'll have him, answers questions honestly, and leaves before dawn. He will appear again.

### Faction Arrivals (Next 1-3 Sessions)

The following contacts arrive in the days following the PCs' return. The order depends on geography and which factions are closest, but the suggested sequence is:

1. **Davresh Kol-Vahni** arrives first with a Varnathi patrol (1-2 days after). He is polite, commanding, and claims authority over the gem.
2. **Captain Iyelet** arrives by wind-ship shortly after (2-3 days). She offers to buy the gem or broker a deal.
3. **Tahira Kel-Sethune** arrives on foot, alone, travel-worn (3-5 days). She asks to study the gem.
4. **Naqqara** sends a letter via dream-courier (a small bird carrying a sealed scroll), requesting a meeting in Asmarat.
5. An **Aram-Zaneth agent** (not Issar-Zul -- he's operating elsewhere) attempts to steal the gem. If this fails, they leave a message: *"The Chained Sun offers what the others cannot: the right to choose."*

The PCs must decide what to do. This decision -- and its consequences -- drives the rest of Act I.

## DM Notes

### Pacing

Session 1 should feel like a slow burn that becomes a controlled panic. The first hour of play should be unsettling but not alarming. The second hour should be increasingly wrong. The journey to the ruin should feel like the PCs are walking toward something they can't turn away from.

Do not rush the village section. The anomalies work because the players have time to notice them, question them, and realize no one else can see the problem. The horror is not the anomalies themselves -- it's the isolation of being the only ones who know something is wrong.

### The Dream-Touched Reveal

The PCs are Dream-Touched, but this term should not be used in session 1. What happens instead is that the PCs *notice* the anomalies while no one else does. This is strange, and the PCs should wonder why. The answer -- that their fates were never finished, making them immune to overwriting -- comes later, likely from Tahira or Khelash, in session 2 or 3.

In session 1, the PCs just know they're different. They don't know why. That uncertainty is part of the hook.

### What If the PCs Ignore the Ruin?

They won't. But if they try, the anomalies in Tal-Kessar continue to worsen. By the end of the second day, people are disappearing. By the third day, the village itself begins to flicker -- buildings phase in and out, the well produces brass-flecked water, and the singing is loud enough to prevent sleep. The ruin is the only lead. The desert is the only direction that makes sense.

If the PCs still won't go, Khelash arrives early and says: "The thing in the sand is rewriting this place. If you do not go to it, it will come to you. It is already coming."
