## Personality

The Ember-Saint is the strangest of the efreeti warlords because he isn't really a warlord at all. He commands a host of mirror-genies and fire-wraiths not through conquest but through philosophical persuasion -- they follow him because he articulated something they'd felt for millennia but couldn't name: **fate was a prison, and the Furnace was its warden.**

He speaks in a register that shifts between exhausted philosopher and furious revolutionary, sometimes mid-sentence. He is prone to long silences, followed by declarations that sound like scripture. He has spent centuries thinking about freedom and has arrived at a conclusion that terrifies even him: if fate is truly broken, then nothing he does matters -- and if nothing matters, then everything is permitted -- and if everything is permitted, then *choice itself* is the only sacred act remaining.

He does not want to rebuild the Brass City. He does not want to restore fate. He wants to **burn what's left of the Furnace** so thoroughly that no one can ever enslave the future again. This makes him an ally to the PCs in some configurations and a catastrophic threat in others.

He has a scar on his chest from the fall of the Brass City -- a wound that never healed, where the Furnace's light cracked him open. It leaks pale radiance instead of fire. He considers it the most honest part of himself.

## Motivation

The Ember-Saint wants the permanent destruction of the Celestine Furnace and all mechanisms of deterministic fate. He does not care about political power, territory, or worship. He cares about one thing: ensuring that no being -- mortal, divine, or elemental -- ever controls the future again.

This puts him at odds with Sirr al-Nar (who wants to control the Furnace), the Myrians (who want to restore it), and even the Aram-Zaneth (who want to rewrite fate, which still requires a mechanism of control). His only natural allies are the Zifari and PCs who choose the "destroy fate" path.

However: if the PCs pursue the Zaneth path ("fate as mutable suggestion"), the Ember-Saint must decide whether a *gentle* form of fate is acceptable or whether even that is too much. This is his crisis point.

## Relationship to the PCs

The Ember-Saint does not seek the PCs out. They find him -- in the deep desert, in a ruin, in a place where the wind carries the smell of cold ash. He tests them with questions, not combat. "If you could erase one future and guarantee another, would you? What gives you that right? What if you're wrong?"

If the PCs' answers interest him, he becomes a powerful but unreliable ally. He will fight Sirr al-Nar gladly. He will share information about the Furnace's inner workings (he was there when it ran). But he will also attempt to destroy prophecy-gems if given the chance, even ones the PCs need.

If the PCs try to restore or claim the Furnace, he opposes them with genuine sorrow.

## Hooks

- He is one of the few beings alive who witnessed the Celestine Furnace in operation and can describe its true nature
- His Fate-Scar ability makes him uniquely valuable in the final dungeon, where fate-effects are overwhelming
- His mirror-genie host is a potential army for the PCs in Act III -- or an enemy
- His philosophical crisis regarding the Zaneth path is a mirror of the campaign's central question
- Other efreeti warlords (Sultana Hadranah, Sul Nashir, Malqizad) view him as a traitor; this creates triangulated efreeti politics
- He has not granted a wish in 200 years. If a PC earns his genuine respect, he may offer one -- and mean it as a gift, not a transaction. This should feel monumental.
